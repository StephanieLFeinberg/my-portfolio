# Portfolio Storytelling & Content Standards

Use this skill when writing, reviewing, or revising case study content for my portfolio.
The goal: case studies that read as Staff-level product design work, not just "what I built."
Apply smart brevity principles: lead with the point, cut anything that doesn't earn its place,
no padding to hit a word count.

## Reference examples

**Layer Lint case study (mchiu.co.uk)** — strong on reasoning transparency. Opens with the
actual technical tension instead of a mission statement. Walks through real implementation
decisions and explains why, not just what (e.g. explicitly naming a boundary the tool
respects and why that boundary matters). Closes by linking to a second case study to show a
throughline across projects, not just a list of unrelated work. Restrained on hype language.

**Explora case study (sanvithi.com)** — strong on structure and quantified impact. Clean,
consistent problem → goals → process → solution → reflection scaffold. Leads with hard
numbers before the narrative even starts. Includes a genuine "what I'd do next" section,
showing forward thinking rather than treating the project as closed. Also good: naming
distinct "key insights" from research rather than a vague summary of "user needs."

Borrow the reasoning transparency from Layer Lint and the structural discipline + quantified
framing from Explora. Avoid Explora's tendency toward repeated/inflated phrasing ("empowers,"
restated headlines) — keep language tight per the standards below.

## What a strong case study includes

- **A real problem, stated plainly.** Not "we wanted to improve X" but the actual tension:
  what was broken, for whom, and how we knew (data, research, a specific moment).
- **The why behind the work, named explicitly.** What triggered this project? A drop in
  conversion, a user research finding, a support ticket pattern, a leadership mandate? Don't
  let the origin stay implicit — state it as its own beat in the story.
- **My role and reasoning, not just outputs.** What decisions did I make and why?
  What tradeoffs did I weigh? Reviewers care more about how I think than the final screens.
- **At least one moment of disagreement or complexity.** A stakeholder pushback, a failed
  direction, a constraint I had to design around. Case studies that are frictionless read as
  either dishonest or shallow.
- **Evidence of influence upstream of the brief, where true.** Did I shape the problem
  statement, push back on scope, or was I handed a fully-formed brief? Say which, honestly.
- **Impact on the user or the business, stated explicitly.** Not implied, not buried, called
  out as its own line. User impact (time saved, confusion reduced, task completion) and/or
  business impact (conversion, retention, cost, adoption). A specific number is stronger than
  a vague claim, but if no hard number exists, a specific qualitative outcome still counts,
  as long as it's framed as impact and not just "shipped."
- **User testing and vibe-coding skills, surfaced where they were part of the process.** If I
  tested with real users or used vibe-coding/AI-assisted prototyping to get to a decision
  faster, name that explicitly as part of the method, not just a footnote.
- **Craft visible in the writing itself.** Tight sentences, no jargon-stuffing, no vague
  inflation ("revolutionized," "seamless," "elevated," "empowers"). If the copy overclaims,
  the work looks like it needs to overclaim.
- **A forward-looking close, where it fits.** What I'd do next, or what I took away, shows
  the thinking didn't stop at ship.

## Standards checklist (run every case study against this)

- [ ] Does the opening line state a real problem, not a mission statement?
- [ ] Is the why behind the work named explicitly (a metric drop, a research finding, a
      specific trigger)? Not just "we decided to look into X."
- [ ] Is my specific role clear (not "we" for everything)?
- [ ] Is there at least one real tradeoff, constraint, or disagreement named?
- [ ] Is impact on the user and/or the business stated explicitly, as its own line, not
      implied?
- [ ] If user testing happened, is it named as part of the process (not just "validated with
      users")?
- [ ] If vibe-coding/AI-assisted prototyping was used, is it named as a method, not omitted?
- [ ] Have I cut every instance of inflated language (revolutionize, seamless, elevate,
      empower, unlock, robust) unless it's doing real work in that sentence?
- [ ] Would a Staff-level reviewer learn something about how I think, not just what shipped?
- [ ] Is every sentence earning its place? (Smart brevity, not a target word count.)
- [ ] Is there a throughline connecting this project to my other work or my broader POV?

## Gaps: never fill with fluff

If any of the above is missing from what I've given you — whether it's the "why," the impact,
the user testing detail, or anything else — do not invent it and do not paper over it with
generic language. Explicitly call out the gap: name which element is missing, and ask me for
the real detail, or flag it as an open item in the draft (e.g. "[IMPACT NEEDED: what changed
for the business/user after this shipped?]"). Vague filler that sounds like an answer but isn't
one is worse than a visible blank, since it reads as confident until someone asks a follow-up
question I can't answer.

## Common pitfalls to flag

- **The "case study as press release."** Everything went smoothly, every decision was
  obviously correct in hindsight. Flag this and ask what actually got debated or revised.
- **Outputs without reasoning.** A wall of final screens with captions, but no decision trail.
- **Borrowed confidence language.** Copy that sounds like it's trying to convince the reader
  rather than show the reader. Specificity is more convincing than adjectives.
- **No visible point of view.** If the case study could have been written by anyone on the
  team, it's not doing its job of showing my design thinking specifically.
- **Restated headlines.** Saying the same claim twice in different words (a trap even strong
  reference examples fall into). Say it once, well.

## When reviewing a draft

Give direct, specific feedback tied to the checklist above, not generic encouragement.
If a section is weak, say which line and why, and suggest what real detail would strengthen it
(a specific number, a specific pushback, a specific decision point) rather than rewriting it
into more inflated language.
