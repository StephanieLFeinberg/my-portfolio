overview: |
  Guild is an education benefit platform that helps working adults build skills for career growth.

  Talent Insights was a new, customer-facing product designed to help employer workforce leaders translate workforce skill gaps into confident learning program recommendations, allowing them to redesign their learning offerings to support their workforce talent goals.

  This project focused on Map Learning, a core concept within Talent Insights.
discoverHeading: "Problem"
discoverBody: |
  Talent Insights didn't start from a blank slate. Guild had already been delivering this as a tech-enabled service — PMs and analysts hand-building these recommendations through decks and one-off analysis. That service proved the demand was real, but it didn't scale. Enough time running it that way had passed for the PM to be confident betting on productizing it: speeding up delivery without losing the delight and trust the manual service had built with employer partners.

  Employer leaders knew they had workforce skill gaps, but struggled to turn that knowledge into specific, confident learning decisions.

  Decisions about education programs Guild offered were:
  - Slow and highly manual
  - Dependent on decks, spreadsheets, and one-off analysis
  - Based heavily on subjective interpretation

  As a result, employers resisted expanding their catalog to match evolving talent needs. Various teams at Guild used **upwards of 6 tools** to answer "what programs are best for this Employer Partner?"

  We began with open-ended discovery to understand how strategic talent decisions were made.

  **Who we talked to**

  **4 Senior Talent Acquisition leaders who:**
  - Own hiring strategy at scale
  - Influence budget
  - Think long-term about workforce planning

  **What keeps them up at night:**
  - Hiring demand outpacing supply
  - Keeping skills current as technology evolves
  - "Hidden" internal talent going unused

  **Key insights**

  Two main areas surfaced from our discovery that Employer Partners care about.

  **"What skills do we have vs need?"**
  - Employers already track this internally
  - Any solution should integrate with their existing systems
  - This felt known to them — and not where urgency was highest

  **"What learning programs should we offer?"**
  - Skills matter more than credentials
  - Strong interest in seeing the skills taught
  - Need to export a succinct summary to share with stakeholders
defineHeading: "Opportunity"
defineBody: |
  Create a software-led decision support experience that:
  - Connects workforce **skill gaps** to relevant **learning programs**
  - Provides the **"why"** behind each recommendation

  Helps leaders make faster, evidence-backed decisions

  **Strategic decision**

  **Focus on learning program recommendations** because that's where business value is concentrated.

  Catalog redesigns took 4+ months and required significant manual effort. Since Guild's revenue scales with program adoption, accelerating this cycle was a direct growth lever. Map Learning aimed to reduce time-to-decision and operational lift.

  The core requirements began to take shape from here, enabling me to prototype a solution we could actually ship.

  **Hypothesis**

  If we replace a **consultative, deck-driven workflow** with a **program recommendation software experience**, employer partners will make **faster, more confident decisions** about their learning catalogs.

  **By:**
  - Highlighting skills gaps at a glance
  - Providing clear rationale behind each recommendation
  - Giving leaders a shareable, executive-ready output
developHeading: "Ideate & Validate"
developBody: |
  With a hard two-week constraint for the internal workflow, I optimized for learning over polish.

  **AI tooling for fast design exploration**

  Rather than investing in high-fidelity Figma specs that might become obsolete, I used Cursor to prototype interaction logic directly with engineers. This enabled rapid feasibility validation and collapsed the traditional design–handoff cycle into a shared working session.

  Once the operational workflow was validated, I shifted back to Figma for the employer-facing experience, where visual precision and brand credibility were critical. AI tools at the time lacked the polish required for executive audiences, so I intentionally separated speed-driven prototyping from brand-driven refinement.

  Different audiences, different constraints, different tools.

deliverHeading: "Validation & Outcome"
deliverBody: |
  The internal curation tool shipped and found real traction: 3 internal teams adopted it, reporting faster time-to-delivery on catalog recommendations to their employer partner contacts, and enough ease-of-use to consolidate what used to take 3 separate tools into one.

  The employer-facing, self-service experience didn't clear the same bar. Validation revealed a critical gap:
  - The tool duplicated existing spreadsheet workflows
  - It didn't introduce enough net-new data to justify switching
  - Employer partners were entrenched in spreadsheet-based processes — switching was measured against that habit, not just usability

  As a result, the employer-facing experience did not move to market. The real unlock from this project turned out to be internal, not external.

  **Reflections**

  This project reshaped how I think about where a software vision's "unlock" actually lives.

  I'd built the employer-facing experience on the assumption that easier and faster would be enough to shift employer partners off spreadsheets. It wasn't — the switching bar was new data, not a better interface, and spreadsheets were too embedded in their existing process to compete with on ease alone.

  If I did this again, I'd:
  - Test employer partners' real switching bar (new data vs. easier workflow) before building the self-service experience, not after
  - Treat spreadsheet-embedded processes as an entrenched competitor, not a gap waiting to be filled
  - Let the internal tool's early traction shape where to invest next, rather than treating it as a stepping stone to the external product

  **Key takeaway**

  Easier isn't always enough to unseat an entrenched workflow — the value has to be new, not just faster. In this case, the real unlock was internal efficiency, not the external self-service vision I set out to build.

  **Why this project matters**
  - I made and owned high-impact scoping decisions
  - I learned that ease-of-use alone doesn't overcome an entrenched workflow — differentiation has to be substantive
  - I saw firsthand how the "internal" build ended up delivering more value than the customer-facing vision it was meant to support

  I took this into future projects at Guild: testing an audience's real switching bar before building around convenience, and treating internal-tooling wins as real outcomes, not consolation prizes.
